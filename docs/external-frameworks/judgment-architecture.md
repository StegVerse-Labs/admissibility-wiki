## Core problem

Judgment Architecture treats judgment as infrastructure rather than an individual trait that can be invoked only when a consequential decision appears.

Its central operational concern is the **Condition Gap**:

```text
execution capacity scales rapidly
while human interpretive capacity scales more slowly
```

Under this condition, a system can preserve visible structure while quietly substituting procedural correctness for actual judgment. Pipelines may remain clean, transitions valid, and outputs enforceable even when the conditions required for interpretation, contest, and accountable commitment have degraded.

## Four-part unit

The framework proposes a repeating decision structure at every organizational level:

1. **Execution Plane** — action occurs within an explicitly bounded domain.
2. **Threshold Triggers** — objective conditions force a hold, pause, or escalation.
3. **Judgment Authority** — a named role can slow, reframe, refuse, or veto.
4. **Accountability Ledger** — contemporaneous evidence records framing, alternatives, criteria, authority, and ownership at commitment.

The scope and consequence change across organizational levels; the structural pattern remains stable.

## Commitment boundary

A key distinction is between automated generation and human commitment.

```text
candidate output != committed action
advisory proposal != authority-bearing transition
silence != adoption
automated generation != accountable commitment
```

Before commitment, an output remains revisable and non-binding. At commitment, authority and accountability attach to a named decision-maker and a real-world transition is authorized.

This boundary can be modeled as a state transition:

```text
reversible advisory state
-> explicit commitment event
-> authority-bearing state mutation
```

The operational risk is not only incorrect execution. It is an unobserved slide across this boundary through momentum, default behavior, temporal compression, or ambiguous delegation.

## Required properties of a governed commitment boundary

A commitment boundary should remain:

- **legible** — the proposal-to-commitment transition is visible at the moment it occurs;
- **interruptible** — authorized refusal or halt remains possible before consequence propagates;
- **attributable** — the authority basis and accountable actor are named;
- **receipt-bearing** — contemporaneous evidence records why the commitment was allowed;
- **contestable** — a defined path exists to challenge or revise the resulting action;
- **recoverable** — reversal conditions and loss boundaries are known before commitment.

## Procedural correctness versus judgment preservation

Judgment Architecture exposes a failure class in which formal structure survives while interpretive grounding disappears.

```text
valid transition != grounded judgment
procedural compliance != preserved interpretive capacity
successful execution != legitimate commitment
recorded approval != current admissibility
```

A system may therefore be internally correct and still produce a resulting state that should not have been admitted under the operative evidence, authority, policy, delegation, timing, or recoverability conditions.

## Interoperability questions

Further research should test:

1. How can a continuously operating system preserve a legible commitment boundary when it produces thousands of candidate actions per second?
2. Which transitions require direct human commitment, and which may proceed under previously bounded delegation?
3. How is judgment capacity measured without reducing judgment to another procedural score?
4. What evidence proves that refusal was practically available rather than merely documented?
5. How are temporal compression, attention depletion, and interpretive overload represented in a commitment record?
6. When does a formally valid transition become inadmissible because the resulting state is no longer grounded in current reality?
7. What recovery mechanism applies when authority remains formally present but has become operationally unusable?
