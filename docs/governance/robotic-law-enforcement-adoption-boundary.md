# Robotic Law-Enforcement Adoption Boundary

## Purpose

Automation may reduce some forms of fatigue, inconsistency, panic, exposure, and replacement cost in safety-critical work. That possibility does not make robotic law enforcement admissible by default, and evidence of human enforcement failure does not itself authorize automated replacement.

This doctrine separates three questions that are often collapsed:

```text
machines may perform a task more consistently
  != robotic deployment is publicly legitimate

human enforcement failed
  != autonomous replacement is admissible

procurement is available
  != procurement authority includes lethal authority
```

## Human failure is not replacement authority

Documented human failure can justify investigation, reform, training, accountability, or narrowly bounded assistance. It does not itself transfer public authority to an automated system, authorize autonomous coercion, or remove the requirement for current policy, consent, oversight, and a reachable refusal point.

## Crisis exploitation

A government or vendor must not use avoidable disorder, injury, fear, or institutional failure to manufacture necessity, accelerate procurement, weaken review, or normalize authority that would not otherwise be admissible.

## Governing concern

A government must not create, tolerate, prolong, or commercially exploit avoidable disorder, injury, or loss of life to manufacture demand for automated enforcement.

Crisis may reveal a genuine safety failure. It may not be treated as self-authenticating authority for expanded surveillance, robotic policing, autonomous weapons, or emergency procurement.

```text
observed chaos != proof of necessity
fear-driven consent != durable public consent
contract award != public-safety legitimacy
operational efficiency != admissibility
```

## Human firearm safety and federal firearm safety

The safety principle does not change when the person holding the firearm acts under federal authority.

Civilian gun-safety rules attempt to constrain possession, handling, storage, and use because a firearm can convert error, anger, fear, or poor judgment into irreversible harm. Federal law-enforcement safety rules should apply the same risk logic to armed agents, while imposing a higher accountability burden because the agent acts with public authority.

Where a person is subdued, disarmed, restrained by multiple agents, or otherwise no longer presents an imminent lethal threat, the state must retain a reachable refusal point before any further lethal force can be applied.

```text
badge != exemption from firearm safety
federal authority != immunity from use-of-force constraints
subdued subject != continuing lethal target
internal policy != independently enforceable accountability
```

## Adoption boundary

Robotic or AI-assisted law enforcement may advance only through bounded, staged authority.

### Stage 0 — Observation and evidence preservation

Permitted functions may include scene mapping, environmental sensing, translation, recording, evidence integrity, and non-coercive alerts.

No independent detention, targeting, weapon control, or force authority is permitted.

### Stage 1 — Human-protective assistance

Permitted functions may include hazardous-material inspection, bomb disposal, search-and-rescue support, remote delivery of medical supplies, and physical shielding.

The system must reduce exposure without acquiring authority over a person.

### Stage 2 — Reversible nonlethal assistance

Any coercive capability must be reversible, independently auditable, time-bounded, and subject to a named human authority able to return `DENY` or `FAIL_CLOSED` before consequence attaches.

### Stage 3 — Prohibited autonomous lethal authority

A robotic system must not independently identify a person as a lethal target, decide that lethal force is justified, or initiate lethal force.

No model score, threat classification, vendor certification, agency approval, or emergency declaration may silently acquire this authority.

## Required admissibility record

Before a robotic enforcement deployment can be considered, the public record must bind at least:

1. the public-safety problem being addressed;
2. evidence that less coercive alternatives were assessed;
3. the system's exact permitted and prohibited actions;
4. the named institution and accountable human authority;
5. current policy and statutory references;
6. procurement beneficiaries and conflicts of interest;
7. training-data, model, sensor, and failure limitations;
8. the reachable pre-consequence refusal point;
9. shutdown, override, recovery, and evidence-preservation paths;
10. independent investigation and civil-liability routes;
11. the validity window and conditions requiring renewed consent;
12. receipts for every material authorization, denial, override, and use of force.

## Anti-profiteering safeguards

A safety crisis must not become an unreviewable revenue bridge between public authority and private vendors.

At minimum, deployment governance should require:

- public disclosure of material ownership, investment, referral, lobbying, and subcontracting interests;
- independent cost and necessity review;
- prohibition on compensation tied to arrests, threat classifications, force events, or expanded deployment volume;
- no proprietary-secret claim that prevents investigation of a consequential decision;
- immutable procurement and authorization receipts;
- automatic expiration rather than permanent normalization of emergency authority;
- personal and institutional consequences for intentional concealment or falsification.

## Accountability rule

Automation relocates human responsibility; it does not remove it.

Responsibility must remain attributable across:

```text
policy author
procurement authority
agency commander
system operator
model and sensor provider
maintenance authority
incident reviewer
execution-authority holder
```

A system cannot absorb blame on behalf of the people and institutions that designed, purchased, authorized, deployed, or failed to stop it.

## Public proof-path test

A legitimate deployment should be able to answer, in public and before use:

> What exact harm is this system allowed to prevent, what is it forbidden to do, who can still stop it, who profits from it, and who is legally answerable when it causes harm?

If those answers cannot be reconstructed from durable records, the deployment is not admissible.

## Boundary statement

```text
robotic capability != robotic authority
lower statistical risk != automatic legitimacy
human failure evidence != replacement authority
crisis procurement != durable consent
vendor certification != execution authority
remote force != reduced accountability
automation != moral distance
```

The governing objective is not to prevent all robotic assistance. It is to ensure that machines arrive through demonstrated safety, public consent, bounded authority, auditability, and enforceable accountability—not through manufactured necessity, tolerated loss of life, or profit extracted from chaos.
